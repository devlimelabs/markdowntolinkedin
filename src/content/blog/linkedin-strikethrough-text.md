You’ve seen it before. A post that crosses out a word or a sentence, like a little inside joke or a quick correction. It’s a cool effect. But when you go to write your own LinkedIn post, you find there’s no button for it. No menu option. Nothing.

So, is it even possible to use strikethrough text on LinkedIn? Yes, it is. But it’s not a feature; it’s a workaround. And it comes with some important warnings.

This guide explains exactly how to create strikethrough text, the best ways to use it for professional posts, and the critical mistakes to avoid so you don’t hurt your post’s visibility.

### What Is Strikethrough and Why Doesn’t LinkedIn Have It?

Strikethrough is simply a line drawn through text. Traditionally, it’s used to show that something has been removed or is wrong. Think of an editor marking up a paper document. It’s a visual sign of deletion.

LinkedIn’s post editor is intentionally simple. It’s designed for clean, professional communication, not complex formatting. That’s why there are no built-in options for different fonts, colors, or styles like strikethrough. The platform prioritizes a uniform look for its feed.

But you can get around this limitation by using Unicode.

### How Strikethrough Text Works (It’s a Trick)

The secret is that you aren’t actually applying a formatting style. You are using special **Unicode characters** that just happen to look like letters with a line through them.

Unicode is the global standard for text characters, and it includes thousands of symbols beyond the basic alphabet. One of these is the “Combining Long Stroke Overlay” (`U+0336`). When you place this character after a letter, it adds a line through it. So, `L` + `̶` becomes `L̶`.

A strikethrough text generator does this for you automatically. It takes your normal text and inserts the combining character after every single letter.

### When Should You Use Strikethrough on LinkedIn?

Just because you can, doesn’t always mean you should. Strikethrough is a niche effect. It works best when used with clear intent. Here are a few good use cases.

*   **To Show a Correction or Update:** This is the most professional use. It’s a transparent way to show you’ve updated information without deleting the original context.
    *   Example: “Our webinar is on Friday at ~~10 AM~~ 11 AM EST.”
    *   Example: “Price: ~~$150~~ Now only $99 for a limited time!”

*   **For Humor and Personality:** Strikethrough can be used to add a layer of commentary or a humorous aside. It’s like whispering a thought to your audience.
    *   Example: “I’m so ~~tired of~~ excited for this week’s project.”
    *   Example: “My new favorite productivity hack is ~~procrastinating~~ planning my day in detail.”

*   **To Show a Completed Task:** In a less formal to-do list, it can be a fun way to show progress.
    *   Example: “My goals for this week:
        *   ~~Finalize the report~~
        *   ~~Email the client~~
        *   Start the next project”

### The Big Warning: Accessibility and SEO Risks

This is the most important part of the guide. Using Unicode for formatting has two major downsides that you must consider.

1.  **Accessibility:** People with visual impairments often use screen readers to navigate the web. These tools read the content of a page out loud. Screen readers often cannot interpret Unicode formatting correctly. The strikethrough text might be skipped entirely, or it might be read out as a confusing string of character names. If the crossed-out text is important, you are excluding part of your audience.

2.  **Search Visibility (SEO):** LinkedIn’s search function is designed to find standard text. It may not recognize these special Unicode characters as regular letters. This means if you use strikethrough on a keyword, **your post might not appear in search results for that term.**

**The rule is simple: Never use strikethrough on important keywords or essential information.**
