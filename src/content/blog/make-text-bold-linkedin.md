Want to make your LinkedIn posts pop? Bolding text is a great way to add emphasis, structure your content, and grab your reader’s attention. But what if you don’t want to use a third-party tool every time?

You’re in luck. It’s possible to make text bold on LinkedIn manually, without any special software. It just takes a little copy-paste magic.

This guide shows you exactly how to do it. We’ll also cover the hidden downsides of this method and introduce a more efficient workflow for people who write a lot of content.

## How Manual Bold Text Works on LinkedIn

LinkedIn’s post editor doesn’t have a bold button. The platform uses plain text to ensure posts look consistent across all devices, from the newest iPhone to an old Android tablet.

So, how are people getting bold text? They aren’t actually “formatting” the text. They are using **Unicode characters**.

Unicode is a universal standard for text characters. It includes the normal letters you’re typing with now, but it also contains thousands of other symbols, including mathematical alphanumeric symbols that just happen to look like bold letters to us.

When you use a tool to “bold” your text, it’s just swapping standard letters for these look-alike Unicode symbols.

For example, it turns this:
`This is bold`

Into this:
`𝗧𝗵𝗶𝘀 𝗶𝘀 𝗯𝗼𝗹𝗱`

Because these are just text characters, you can copy and paste them into almost any text field on LinkedIn.

## The Step-by-Step Manual Method

Ready to try it yourself? Here’s how to create bold text for LinkedIn without installing any tools.

### Step 1: Open a Unicode Text Converter

First, you need a website that can generate these special characters. There are many free options available. Just search for “Unicode text generator” or “bold text generator.” YayText and LingoJam are two popular choices.

### Step 2: Type and Convert Your Text

In the generator tool, type the word or phrase you want to make bold. The site will automatically show you that text in dozens of different styles.

### Step 3: Copy the Bold Style You Want

Look for a clean, readable bold style. You’ll usually see options like:

*   **Sans-serif bold** (clean and modern)
*   **Serif bold** (more traditional, with “feet” on the letters)

Click the “Copy” button next to the style you prefer. This saves the Unicode characters to your clipboard.

### Step 4: Paste it into LinkedIn

Now, just go to your LinkedIn post, comment, or profile and paste (Ctrl+V or Cmd+V) the text where you want it. It should appear bold right in the editor.

You can use this method almost anywhere on LinkedIn, including:

*   Posts and articles
*   Comments
*   Your profile headline and “About” section
*   Your “Experience” section descriptions
*   Direct messages

It seems simple, right? It is. But this manual method has some serious drawbacks you need to know about.

## The Problems with Unicode Bold Text

Using these special characters isn’t a perfect solution. In fact, it can cause significant problems with accessibility and searchability.

### 1. It Breaks Screen Readers

This is the biggest issue. People with visual impairments often use screen reader software to read web content aloud. Screen readers don’t recognize these look-alike Unicode characters as letters. Instead, they might read them out as their official Unicode names (“mathematical bold capital T,” “mathematical bold small h”) or, even worse, just say “blank.”

Using fake bold text can make your content completely unreadable for a portion of your audience. As accessibility advocate Luise Freese puts it, “If it only works for sighted users, it doesn’t really work.”

### 2. It’s Invisible to LinkedIn Search

LinkedIn’s search engine doesn’t see these Unicode symbols as regular text either. If you make a keyword bold, **your post will not show up in search results for that keyword.**

This is a huge problem for discoverability. You should never use Unicode formatting on important terms, like:

*   Core keywords for your industry
*   Your name or company name
*   Hashtags
*   Job titles

### 3. It Can Look Broken on Some Devices

While most modern devices can display these characters correctly, some older operating systems (especially on Android) can’t. On those devices, your bolded text might just appear as a series of empty boxes (☐☐☐).
