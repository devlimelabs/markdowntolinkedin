### The Manual Method: Using Unicode Characters

LinkedIn posts don't support standard formatting like you'd find in Microsoft Word or Google Docs. There's no `Ctrl+B` shortcut. When you see bold text in a post, you aren't seeing formatted text at all. You're seeing special Unicode characters.

Unicode is a standard that assigns a unique code to every character, symbol, and emoji. Deep within this system, there are characters that look like bold versions of the regular alphabet. For example, the standard letter 'a' is a different character from the mathematical bold '𝐚'.

To add them to your post manually, you would have to:

1.  Open a Unicode character finder website.
2.  Type your desired text.
3.  Find and copy the bold-looking Unicode equivalent.
4.  Paste it directly into your LinkedIn post.

It works. Your text will look bold. But this approach is a bad idea.

### Why You Must Avoid Manual Unicode Formatting

Using these "fake bold" characters creates serious problems for your content and your audience. It's a formatting hack that can do more harm than good.

**1. It Breaks Accessibility**

People who are visually impaired often use screen readers to navigate the web. These devices read content aloud. When a screen reader encounters a standard letter 'B', it says "B". When it encounters the Unicode character '𝐁', it might say "Mathematical Bold Capital B" or, even worse, nothing at all. 

As accessibility expert Luise Freese puts it, using these characters is like building a wall that blocks a portion of your audience from reading your message. Your clever formatting trick completely vanishes for anyone relying on assistive tech.

**2. It Kills Your Search Visibility**

LinkedIn's search function, and Google's for that matter, doesn't recognize these special Unicode characters as standard text. If you write a post about "**𝐩𝐫𝐨𝐝𝐮𝐜𝐭𝐢𝐯𝐢𝐭𝐲**" using fake bold, your post will not appear in search results for the keyword "productivity".

By trying to make your keywords stand out visually, you make them invisible to the platform's search algorithm. You're sacrificing reach for a purely cosmetic change.

**3. It Can Look Broken on Some Devices**

Older devices, particularly some Android phones, can't always render these special characters correctly. Instead of bold text, your audience might just see a series of empty boxes (☐☐☐). While this issue is becoming less common, it still creates a poor user experience for a segment of your readers.

### A Better Way: Write in Markdown

So, if the manual method is out, what's the solution? The answer is to change your writing process slightly by using Markdown.

Markdown is a simple way to write formatted text using plain characters. It was designed to be easy to read and easy to write. Instead of complex codes, you use simple symbols you already know.

To make text bold in Markdown, you just wrap it in two asterisks. It's that simple.

`**This text will be bold.**`

This approach keeps your original text clean, searchable, and accessible. You're not using weird characters; you're just adding simple markers to indicate formatting.
