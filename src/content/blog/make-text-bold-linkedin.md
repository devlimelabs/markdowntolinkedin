### The Manual Method: Creating "Bold" Text with Unicode

So, how do people make text look bold without a button? They use Unicode characters. Unicode is the global standard that gives every letter, number, and symbol a unique code. This includes thousands of symbols that look like bold, italic, or even cursive letters.

Here’s how you can do it manually:

1.  **Find a Unicode Text Generator:** Search for a "bold text generator" or "fancy text generator" online.
2.  **Enter Your Text:** Type the word or phrase you want to make bold into the generator.
3.  **Copy the Unicode Version:** The tool will output your text in various styles. Find the bold style you like and copy it.
4.  **Paste into LinkedIn:** Paste the copied characters directly into your LinkedIn post, comment, or even your profile headline.

It looks bold. Simple, right? Not quite. What you’ve just pasted isn’t actually bold text; it’s a string of symbols that just happen to resemble bold letters.

And that’s where the problems begin.

### The Hidden Dangers of Unicode Formatting

Using Unicode characters for formatting is a risky shortcut. It can hurt your post's reach and exclude parts of your audience.

#### 1. It's Invisible to Screen Readers

This is the most critical issue. Millions of people with visual impairments use screen readers to browse the web. These tools read on-screen content aloud. When a screen reader encounters standard text, it reads it perfectly. When it encounters Unicode symbols, it often fails.

According to research on accessibility from disability charity Scope, a screen reader might either skip the Unicode characters completely or try to read out their confusing technical names. For example, instead of hearing your key message, a user might hear "mathematical sans-serif bold G" or just silence.

Your bolded text becomes meaningless, leaving a huge gap in your content for anyone using assistive technology.

![An infographic showing a screen reader skipping over Unicode bold text, with a sad face emoji, and reading standard text correctly, with a happy face emoji.](/blog/images/linkedin-bold-accessibility.png)

#### 2. It's Bad for SEO and Search

LinkedIn's internal search engine, just like Google, is designed to index and find standard text. When you use Unicode characters, you're essentially hiding your most important words from search.

If you write a post about **"sales strategies"** using Unicode, and someone searches for "sales strategies" on LinkedIn, your post probably won't show up. You’ve effectively removed your keywords from the index. It's a huge own-goal for your content's visibility.

#### 3. It Can Break on Different Devices

While most modern devices can render Unicode symbols correctly, older operating systems, especially on Android, can't. On these devices, your carefully crafted bold text might just appear as a series of empty boxes (☐☐☐). It looks broken and unprofessional, undermining the very goal of making your post look good.

So if the manual Unicode method is a bad idea, what’s the alternative? The answer is **markdown**.

Markdown is a simple syntax for formatting text. You’ve probably seen it before. To make text bold in markdown, you just wrap it in two asterisks, like this: `**This is bold text**`.
