## What's the Secret? It's Unicode

LinkedIn doesn't have a bold button. So, when you see bold text in a post, you're not actually seeing formatted text. You're seeing **Unicode characters**.

Think of Unicode as a giant library of symbols. It contains every character for every language, plus a ton of other symbols, like emojis and mathematical characters. Hidden in that library are characters that look just like the bold letters of the alphabet.

For example, a normal "B" is one character. But there's another character, "ùêÅ" (Mathematical Bold Capital B), that looks identical but is technically a different symbol. By swapping regular letters for their bold Unicode counterparts, you can create text that appears bold anywhere, including a LinkedIn post.

## The Problems with Using Unicode Text

This method works, but it's important to know the downsides. Because you're using special symbols instead of real formatting, it can cause some problems.

#### 1. It's Bad for Accessibility

This is the biggest issue. People who use screen readers to browse the web will have a poor experience. A screen reader might read the bold text out letter by letter (e.g., "Mathematical Bold Capital H, Mathematical Bold Small E...") or, in some cases, just go silent. This makes your post confusing or completely inaccessible to some users.

#### 2. It Might Not Show Up Correctly

Not all devices and operating systems can render every Unicode character. On some older devices, your carefully crafted bold text might just appear as a series of empty boxes or question marks (‚òê‚òê‚òê‚òê‚òê).

#### 3. It's Not Searchable

LinkedIn's search function (and Google's) looks for standard text. Since your bold text is made of different characters, it won't show up in search results. If you're trying to rank for a specific keyword, putting it in Unicode bold is a bad idea.
